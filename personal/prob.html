Introduction to Quantitative Genomics

Quantitative genomics combines molecular biology, statistics, and computational methods to understand how genetic variations influence traits and diseases. Unlike traditional genetics that often focuses on single genes, quantitative genomics examines how multiple genetic factors interact with environmental influences to shape complex traits. This guide provides a foundation for understanding the statistical principles and methodologies that drive modern genomic research.

Essential Genomic Concepts

DNA, Genes, and Variants

  • DNA: Double-helix molecule composed of nucleotides (A, T, G, C)
  • Genes: Sections of DNA that code for proteins
  • Single Nucleotide Polymorphisms (SNPs): Single base-pair variations in DNA
  • Alleles: Alternative forms of a gene or genetic locus
  • Genotype: The genetic makeup of an individual
  • Phenotype: Observable traits resulting from genotype and environment

Genetic Variation Statistics

Type of Variation Frequency in Human Genome Typical Size
SNPs 1 per 300 base pairs 1 base pair
Insertions/Deletions (Indels) ~1 million per genome 1-50 base pairs
Copy Number Variations (CNVs) ~100-1000 per genome 1,000+ base pairs
Structural Variations ~20-50 per genome 50,000+ base pairs

Hardy-Weinberg Equilibrium (HWE)

A fundamental principle in population genetics that describes the relationship between allele and genotype frequencies in a population.

\[p^2 + 2pq + q^2 = 1\]

Where:

  • \(p\) = frequency of the dominant allele
  • \(q\) = frequency of the recessive allele
  • \(p^2\) = frequency of homozygous dominant genotype
  • \(2pq\) = frequency of heterozygous genotype
  • \(q^2\) = frequency of homozygous recessive genotype

Example: If allele A has frequency 0.7 and allele a has frequency 0.3 in a population:

  • Genotype AA frequency = \(0.7^2 = 0.49\) (49%)
  • Genotype Aa frequency = \(2 \times 0.7 \times 0.3 = 0.42\) (42%)
  • Genotype aa frequency = \(0.3^2 = 0.09\) (9%)

Genome-Wide Association Studies (GWAS)

Basic GWAS Methodology

GWAS examines associations between genetic variants (typically SNPs) across the genome and specific traits or diseases.

  1. Collect DNA samples from case (affected) and control (unaffected) groups
  2. Genotype hundreds of thousands to millions of SNPs in each individual
  3. Compare allele frequencies between cases and controls for each SNP
  4. Apply statistical tests to identify significant associations
  5. Account for multiple testing (many SNPs tested simultaneously)

Statistical Power in GWAS

Effect Size (Odds Ratio) Sample Size Needed (80% Power) Minor Allele Frequency
1.1 (small) ~10,000-20,000 cases/controls 0.2
1.3 (moderate) ~2,000-5,000 cases/controls 0.2
1.5 (large) ~1,000-2,000 cases/controls 0.2
2.0 (very large) ~500-1,000 cases/controls 0.2

Manhattan Plots

A visualization tool in GWAS showing the statistical significance of associations across the genome.

  • X-axis: Genomic position (chromosomes)
  • Y-axis: Negative logarithm of p-value (-log10(p))
  • Higher points indicate stronger statistical evidence for association
  • Genome-wide significance threshold typically set at p < 5 × 10^-8

Statistical Methods in Genomics

Association Testing

Common statistical tests used in genomic association studies:

Test Application Formula/Method
Chi-Square Test Case-control studies \[\chi^2 = \sum \frac{(O-E)^2}{E}\]
Logistic Regression Binary outcomes with covariates \[\ln\left(\frac{p}{1-p}\right) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_1 + ... + \beta_n X_n\]
Linear Regression Quantitative traits \[Y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_1 + ... + \beta_n X_n + \epsilon\]
Mixed Linear Models Accounting for relatedness/population structure \[Y = X\beta + Zu + \epsilon\]

Multiple Testing Correction

Methods to control false positives when testing millions of variants:

  • Bonferroni correction: \(\alpha_{adjusted} = \frac{\alpha}{n}\) where n is the number of tests
  • False Discovery Rate (FDR): Controls the expected proportion of false positives
  • Permutation testing: Empirical estimation of significance thresholds

Example: Testing 1 million SNPs with desired α = 0.05

  • Bonferroni-corrected threshold: \(0.05 / 1,000,000 = 5 \times 10^{-8}\)
  • This is the standard genome-wide significance threshold in GWAS

Key Calculations and Metrics

Heritability

The proportion of phenotypic variance attributed to genetic factors:

\[h^2 = \frac{\sigma^2_G}{\sigma^2_P} = \frac{\sigma^2_G}{\sigma^2_G + \sigma^2_E}\]

Where:

  • \(h^2\) = heritability
  • \(\sigma^2_G\) = genetic variance
  • \(\sigma^2_P\) = phenotypic variance
  • \(\sigma^2_E\) = environmental variance

SNP-Based Heritability

Trait Type Typical SNP-Based Heritability Range Example Traits
Highly heritable 70-90% Height, eye color
Moderately heritable 40-70% BMI, blood pressure
Low heritability 10-40% Many complex diseases
Very low heritability <10% Certain environmental diseases

Effect Size Metrics

Common measures of genetic effect magnitude:

  • Odds Ratio (OR): Ratio of odds of disease given exposure to odds of disease given no exposure
  • Relative Risk (RR): Ratio of risk of disease in exposed group to risk in unexposed group
  • Beta (β): Linear regression coefficient indicating the change in trait per allele
  • Variance Explained: Proportion of phenotypic variance explained by a variant

Variance explained by a single SNP:

\[V_{explained} = \frac{2 \times MAF \times (1-MAF) \times \beta^2}{Var(phenotype)}\]

Where:

  • MAF = Minor Allele Frequency
  • β = Effect size

Practical Applications

Polygenic Risk Scores (PRS)

A method to predict genetic risk for complex traits by aggregating effects across many variants:

\[PRS = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \beta_i \times SNP_i\]

Where:

  • \(\beta_i\) = effect size of SNP i
  • \(SNP_i\) = genotype of SNP i (typically coded as 0, 1, or 2 alleles)

Example: Calculating a simple PRS for three SNPs:

  • SNP1: Effect size (β) = 0.05, Genotype = 1 → 0.05 × 1 = 0.05
  • SNP2: Effect size (β) = 0.12, Genotype = 2 → 0.12 × 2 = 0.24
  • SNP3: Effect size (β) = 0.03, Genotype = 0 → 0.03 × 0 = 0
  • PRS = 0.05 + 0.24 + 0 = 0.29

Genomic Prediction Accuracy

Metric Formula Interpretation
Area Under the ROC Curve (AUC) Plot of sensitivity vs. 1-specificity 0.5 = random prediction, 1.0 = perfect prediction
R² (Coefficient of Determination) \[R^2 = 1 - \frac{SS_{res}}{SS_{tot}}\] Proportion of variance explained by the model
Pearson Correlation \[r = \frac{cov(X,Y)}{\sigma_X \sigma_Y}\] Linear correlation between predicted and observed values

Advanced Concepts

Linkage Disequilibrium (LD)

The non-random association of alleles at different loci, typically measured as r² or D':

\[D = P_{AB} - P_A \times P_B\]

\[D' = \frac{D}{D_{max}}\]

\[r^2 = \frac{D^2}{P_A \times P_B \times (1-P_A) \times (1-P_B)}\]

Where:

  • \(P_{AB}\) = frequency of haplotype AB
  • \(P_A\) = frequency of allele A
  • \(P_B\) = frequency of allele B

Genetic Architecture Models

Different models of how genetic variants contribute to traits:

Model Description Examples
Common Disease-Common Variant Common variants (MAF > 5%) with small effects Height, BMI
Common Disease-Rare Variant Many rare variants with larger effects Autism, schizophrenia
Infinitesimal Model Many variants with tiny effects Height, cognitive ability
Oligogenic Model Few variants with moderate to large effects Some Mendelian disorders with reduced penetrance

Genome-Wide Complex Trait Analysis (GCTA)

A method to estimate the proportion of phenotypic variance explained by all SNPs:

\[y = X\beta + g + \epsilon\]

Where:

  • \(y\) = phenotype vector
  • \(X\) = matrix of fixed effects
  • \(\beta\) = vector of fixed effects
  • \(g\) = vector of random genetic effects with \(g \sim N(0, G\sigma^2_g)\)
  • \(G\) = genetic relationship matrix
  • \(\epsilon\) = vector of residual effects

Future Directions in Quantitative Genomics

Emerging Areas

  • Multi-omics Integration: Combining genomics with transcriptomics, proteomics, and metabolomics
  • Functional Genomics: Moving from association to causation
  • Pharmacogenomics: Using genetic information to optimize drug response
  • Gene-Environment Interactions: Understanding how genetic effects are modulated by environment
  • Machine Learning Applications: Leveraging AI for complex genomic pattern recognition

Methodological Advances

  • Bayesian Methods: Incorporating prior knowledge into genomic analyses
  • Network-Based Approaches: Analyzing genes in biological pathways and networks
  • Causal Inference: Mendelian randomization and other methods for causal discovery
  • Single-Cell Analytics: Quantitative methods for single-cell genomic data

Quantitative genomics is a rapidly evolving field at the intersection of biology, statistics, and computer science. While the mathematical foundations presented here remain relevant, new methodologies and applications continue to emerge as technology advances. The transition from discovering genetic associations to understanding their functional implications represents the next frontier in genomic research.

Introduction to Poker Probability

Poker is a game of skill, psychology, and mathematics. Understanding the underlying probability theory gives you a significant edge over players who rely solely on intuition. This guide will provide you with the statistical foundations of poker and practical methods for making quick, accurate calculations at the table.

Calculating Poker Odds

Basic Odds Calculation

The formula for calculating odds against an event:

\[\text{Odds Against} = \frac{\text{Number of Unfavorable Outcomes}}{\text{Number of Favorable Outcomes}}\]

Or

\[\text{Odds Against} = \frac{1 - P}{P}\]

Where P is the probability of success.

Converting Between Probability and Odds

From Formula Example
Odds to Probability \(P = \frac{1}{1 + \text{Odds}}\) 3:1 odds = 1/(1+3) = 0.25 or 25%
Probability to Odds \(\text{Odds} = \frac{1-P}{P}\) 25% probability = (1-0.25)/0.25 = 3 or 3:1 odds

The Concept of Outs

"Outs" are the cards that can improve your hand. Calculating your outs is the foundation of poker probability.

Example: If you have 9♥10♥ and the flop is 7♥8♦Q♥, your outs are:

  • Any heart (flush): 9 cards
  • Any Jack (straight): 4 cards
  • Minus overlap (J♥): -1 card
  • Total outs: 12 cards

Expected Value (EV) in Poker

Expected Value is the long-term average outcome of a decision. It's calculated as:

\[EV = (P_{win} \times \text{Win Amount}) - (P_{lose} \times \text{Lose Amount})\]

Where:

  • \(P_{win}\) is the probability of winning
  • \(P_{lose}\) is the probability of losing (equal to \(1 - P_{win}\))

Example EV Calculation

Scenario: You have a flush draw (9 outs) on the flop.

  • The pot is $100
  • Your opponent bets $50
  • You need to call $50 to continue

Calculating EV:

  1. Your probability of hitting by the river is about 35% (using the Rule of 4)
  2. If you hit, you win $150 (current pot + opponent's bet)
  3. If you miss, you lose $50 (your call)

\[EV = (0.35 \times \$150) - (0.65 \times \$50)\]

\[EV = \$52.50 - \$32.50 = \$20\]

Since the EV is positive, this is a profitable call in the long run.

Pot Odds vs. Card Odds

Pot odds are the ratio of the current pot size to the cost of a contemplated call.

\[\text{Pot Odds} = \frac{\text{Current Pot Size}}{\text{Cost to Call}}\]

If your pot odds are greater than your card odds, you have a profitable call.

Example:

  • The pot is $100
  • Your opponent bets $25
  • Pot odds = $125/$25 = 5:1
  • With a flush draw (35% to hit), your card odds are about 2:1
  • Since 5:1 > 2:1, this is a profitable call

Quick Mental Calculation Methods

The Rule of 2 and 4

A simple method to estimate the probability of completing a draw:

  • With one card to come (turn to river): Multiply outs by 2 to get percentage
  • With two cards to come (flop to river): Multiply outs by 4 to get percentage

Example: With 8 outs (open-ended straight draw)

  • Turn probability: 8 × 2 = 16%
  • Flop to river probability: 8 × 4 = 32%

This is a slight overestimation but works well for quick decisions.

The Rule of 4 and 2 (More Accurate)

A more precise version:

  • Multiply outs by 4 for turn probability
  • Multiply outs by 2 for river probability (if you missed on the turn)
  • Add them for combined probability from flop to river

Example: 8 straight draw outs

  • Turn probability: 8 × 4 = 32%
  • River probability (if missed on turn): 8 × 2 = 16%
  • Combined: 32% + (68% × 16%) = 32% + 10.9% = 42.9%

The actual mathematical probability is 31.5%, so this is still an approximation.

Decision Making Based on Statistics

Key Decision Points in Poker

  1. Preflop decisions:
    • Use starting hand charts based on position
    • Adjust based on stack sizes and opponent tendencies
    • Remember that position is worth approximately 1-2 card ranks in value
  2. Postflop with made hands:
    • Calculate the strength of your hand relative to possible opponent holdings
    • Estimate how your hand will perform against calling and raising ranges
  3. Postflop with drawing hands:
    • Calculate pot odds and compare to drawing odds
    • Consider implied odds for future betting rounds
    • Evaluate the disguised value of your hand if you hit

Bankroll Management Using Probability

The Risk of Ruin (RoR) formula for a simplified poker model:

\[\text{RoR} = \left(\frac{1-\text{edge}}{1+\text{edge}}\right)^{\text{bankroll}/\text{buyin}}\]

Where:

  • edge is your win rate as a decimal
  • bankroll is your total poker money
  • buyin is the amount for one game

Example: With a 5% edge and 20 buyins:

\[\text{RoR} = \left(\frac{1-0.05}{1+0.05}\right)^{20} = (0.95/1.05)^{20} \approx 0.36 = 36\%\]

Advanced Concepts

Equity Calculation

Equity is your share of the pot based on your probability of winning.

Example:

  • You have A♠K♠ vs. opponent's Q♥Q♦
  • Your equity is approximately 43%
  • If the pot is $100, your equity is $43

Common equity matchups to memorize:

  • Pair vs. Overcards: approximately 55% vs. 45%
  • Top Pair vs. Flush Draw: approximately 65% vs. 35%
  • Set vs. Two Pair: approximately 75% vs. 25%

Balancing Ranges

A mathematically balanced range contains an optimal ratio of value bets to bluffs:

\[\text{Optimal Bluff Frequency} = \frac{\text{Bet Size}}{\text{Pot Size} + \text{Bet Size}}\]

Example:

  • You bet $75 into a $100 pot
  • Optimal bluff frequency = $75/($100+$75) = 0.43 or 43%
  • Your range should contain 43% bluffs and 57% value hands

Practical Applications

In-Game Calculation Example

Street: Flop

  • Your hand: J♠10♠
  • Board: K♠Q♥2♦
  • Action: Opponent bets $40 into $60 pot

Quick analysis:

  1. You have 8 outs for the straight (any Ace or 9)
  2. Using Rule of 4: 8 × 4 = 32% to hit by the river
  3. Pot odds: $100/$40 = 2.5:1
  4. Converting your chance to odds: (100-32)/32 = 2.1:1
  5. Since 2.5:1 > 2.1:1, this is a profitable call

Tournament Strategy Adjustments

ICM (Independent Chip Model) considerations modify EV calculations in tournaments:

  • Each chip gained has decreasing marginal value
  • Each chip lost has increasing marginal cost
  • This creates a "risk premium" on decisions

\[\text{ICM-adjusted EV} = \text{EV} - \text{Risk Premium}\]

Where Risk Premium increases as:

  • The tournament gets closer to payouts
  • Your stack gets shorter relative to the blinds
  • The pay jumps get larger

Remember that poker is both art and science. These calculations provide a foundation, but factors like player psychology, table image, and specific opponent tendencies will always influence optimal decision-making. The best players combine statistical knowledge with keen observation and adaptability.
